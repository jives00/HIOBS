HIOBS HISTORY

 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 

1964

Peter O. Willauer founds the Hurricane Island Outward Bound School. The summer of 1964 is known as “H-0”.  The first buildings on the island are constructed, trails are cut and course elements perfected with the help of 40 volunteers—both instructors and students—who spent the summer on Hurricane Island.

1965

5 pulling boats are delivered and the teaching staff numbers 22. 170 students, ages 15 to 22+ complete H-1 and H-2, the first public courses of Hurricane Island Outward Bound School. 82 students are on scholarship. Each group takes its turn at "Duty Watch," which includes 24/7 monitoring of radio communications and served the community by firefighting for the Maine Forest Service.

1966

H-3, H-4, and H-5 are run off Hurricane Island; all include one instructor's watch.

1967

H-6, H-7, H-8, H-9 and H-10 take place. The first educators’ courses, as well as the first spring and fall sailing courses are offered. A 50' rescue boat, M/V Hurricane, is built by Southwest Boat Corps. 20 acres of Cross Island are donated to Hurricane Island Outward Bound School.

1968

H-11, H-12, H-13, H-14 are run. Pulling boats 6, 7, 8 and 9 are built. Land-based winter snowshoeing courses take place between December and March near Greenville. Improvements are made to pulling boat masts and rudders. A heated drown-proofing pool is under construction.

1969

The first adult and coed courses run. Pulling boats 10 and 11 are built. Total of 50 staff in mid-summer. H-17 and H-19 are the first mobile courses, operating for approximately 12 days from Bartlett's Island in Blue Hill Bay. H-18 is filmed by Joern Gerdts. The first endowment gift of $88,500 is received. Tuition raised to $500.

1970

First accredited teachers’ courses are offered. A 26-day course, H-29, is offered. 27 students earned 6 graduate credit hours each from the University of Massachusetts School of Education through Hurricane Island Outward Bound School.

1971

The first girls’ course, the first women’s course, and the first co-ed educator courses are run. The first truly mobile course for 2 watches covered the Maine coast, sailing from Monhegan Island, east to Machias and back to Hurricane. Pulling boat 12, Endurance, is built by Chet Rittall of Boothbay Harbor with donations from over 400 alumni. Two 37’, diesel-powered, fiberglass boats, M/V Reliance and M/V Vigilant, begin operating at the end of September.

1972

Pulling boats 13 and 14 are built. Pulling boat 13 is named in memory of David I. Mixter, an H-4 alumnus who lost his life in Vietnam. Dave's family and friends donated the boat's complete construction and equipment as a lasting memorial. Pulling boat 14 bears a plaque in memory of Chapin Wright, an H-36 alumnus from Aspen, Colorado, who was killed in an automobile accident. Chapin’s parents contributed the plaque and his sister helped finance the boat. A Moosehead region winter course is piloted at Grant Farm. The administrative office moved from Concord, MA to a space on Main Street in Rockland, ME.

1973

Property for the Rockland headquarters purchased for $35,000. The first HIOBS courses for executives are run. ABC Television films a documentary at Hurricane Island, BJ and Eddie Outward Bound, during H-50, the first course of the season. The 30-minute film is the first full TV documentary on an American Outward Bound School and is viewed by 10 million people nationwide. The first public winter course, H-47, is open to all alumni—men and women—and is run in January at Grant Farm on the east side of Moosehead Lake in North-Central Maine.

1974

Hurricane Island Outward Bound School 10th year, with 5,700 alumni. For the first time in HIOBS history, all courses are filled with waiting lists. In January, the Maine State Department of Education gives permission to all secondary schools to grant formal credit for HIOBS courses. Three "juniors" courses are added for students 14 to 16-years-old in May, August and September. Burnt Island is established as a base site. Three three-week-long winter courses are offered in January, February and March and are open to all, including alumni. The new Rescue Station on Hurricane begins operating in August, funded in memory of Rick Barnett, an H-37 alumnus. Outward Bound’s founder, Kurt Hahn, dies on December 14, 1974 and is buried in Salem, Germany.

1975

The first STEP (Short Term Elective Program) program for adjudicated youth in Florida is run. The Rockland headquarters construction begins, with Betty Willauer turning the first shovelful of earth. Winter land programs move operations to Bethel, ME. The first courses for women over thirty are offered. The Coast Guard requires watch officers to be licensed; they document the pulling boats as "sailing ketches" and approve their safety. The first Odyssey expedition begins with a group of instructors, lead by Steve Bailey, leaving Hurricane Island on pulling boat 14 on October 7 bound for the Florida Keys. After several stops along the way, the crew arrive in Big Pine Key in late December. This was the first of many Odyssey expeditions that became a traditional long staff training expedition.

1976

After a voyage of 4000 miles, the staff training Odyssey expedition returned to Hurricane Island in early June. With completion of the new operational base facility in the spring, all sailing courses begin and end in Rockland. The new Rockland base houses administrative offices, as well as space for boat storage and maintenance. All winter courses are 10 days long and completely mobile.

1977

HF-1, the first Florida sailing course, ran from November 15 to November 28, 1977. The "adaptive" programs begin to offer the Outward Bound experience to youth at risk.

1978

Pulling boats 15 and 16 are built.

1979

The alumni body now totals 10,618. HIOBS now runs courses from 5 days to 26 days for teens and adults year-round. Pulling boats 17 and 18 are built.

1980

Land is purchased in the Sunday River Valley for the future site of the Leon L. Bean Mountain Center, a land program base facility in Newry, ME.

1981

The summer land program out of Newry is expanded to include public courses. The first semester-length college-accredited course is run.

1982

The first Outward Bound Directive (youth at risk) course takes place. This program is a collaboration with Beech Hill Hospital in Dublin, NH and begins to integrate an Outward Bound experience in the treatment for substance abuse in adolescents.

1983

The Island Institute is founded as a HIOBS affiliate. The first course for Vietnam Veterans is run, a program established by Col. Robert "Bob" Rheault. The program for status offenders, “FINS” (Families in Need of Service), opens in Mims, FL. The Tallahassee, FL office opens. The Northwoods Program begins in the Moosehead Lake region in northern Maine. The first multi-element courses are introduced.

1984

The first sea-kayaking course is run. The Fleet Endowment Challenge begins.

1985

The Leon L. Bean Mountain Center is built in Newry, ME. Long and short Juniors courses and Outward Bound Directive, later named Ascent and then renamed Intercept, expand rapidly. Hurricane Island Outward Bound School alumni number 29,701.

1986

The Baltimore Chesapeake Bay Outward Bound Program (BCBOB) is established. Courses are run for the hearing-impaired. 60 Minutes films the Women of Courage course for female cancer survivors. Peter Willauer receives the Kurt Hahn Award from Outward Bound USA.

1987

The first 6-day diabetic youth sailing course on Hurricane Island is run in partnership with the Joslin Diabetes Center.

1988

The first courses for blind students are offered. The Gorman Staff Building is completed at the Leon L. Bean Mountain Center in Newry, ME. A second pulling boat for the BCBOB Program is launched. A 5-year $3.3 million capital campaign is launched to raise funds for the endowment and the physical plant. HIOBS is the first organization approved by the State of Maine Department of Education as a Non-traditional Limited Purpose School because of a "commitment to the educational process and to the youth of Maine." The Thompson Island Outward Bound Education Center, located in Boston Harbor, is incorporated. The Desoto Start Center on the Peace River in Arcadia, FL is the latest addition to five "special programs" operating in Florida to serve delinquent youth.

1989

HIOBS celebrates its 25th anniversary. HIOBS President and Founder, Peter O. Willauer retires. Stephen Kaagen, PhD, and former Vermont Commissioner of Education, is appointed president.

1990

The Lewis Building is dedicated in Rockland, ME. A lilac is planted by the Rockland Logistics building in memory of logistics manager Scott Rogers and instructor Lianne Ritter, who lost their lives in an auto accident while transporting a boat from Florida to Maine.

1991

Stephen Kaagen, HIOBS president, resigns and Bob Rheault is appointed Interim President as a presidential search commences. Joan Welsh, associate director of the Colorado Outward Bound School, is appointed president. HIOBS alumni exceed 60,000.

1992

Philadelphia Friends of Outward Bound is formed. HIOBS enjoys the support of over 2,500 donors.

1993

HIOBS enrolls 6,228 students in courses. Maine's most prominent civic leaders participate in an innovative program, designed to explore new models for civic leadership, later to become Leadership Maine. Florida Southern Program's adjudicated youth courses expand to include an academic component, which prepares students for their high school diploma while they are attending their Outward Bound course. $1.3 million of debt is retired. Expeditionary Learning Outward Bound is established.

1994

HIOBS celebrates its 30th year. Over 7,000 boys, girls, men and women participate in programs. A diversity plan is completed. Baltimore receives its first diversity grant from the State of Maryland to recruit and train minority staff. Florida's program for adjudicated youth is ranked as one of the five most successful U.S. programs of its kind by a U.S. Department of Justice report on successful intervention programs for juvenile offenders.

1995

STEP in Yulee, FL celebrates its 20th anniversary. An agreement for use of Burnt Island establishes the Maine Sea Program’s “southern foothold".

1996

The Escambia River base for adjudicated youth programs opens in Century, FL. A base in Key Largo, FL is opened to house the expansion of the FINS program. Two ascent courses are run in Ontario, Canada in partnership with the Canadian Outward Bound School. Some 600 field staff are employed in the peak season. The school’s Annual Fund exceeds $1 million this year, for the second time in its history.

1997

The Florida Sea Program celebrates its 20th year of operation. PFOB (Philadelphia Friends of Outward Bound) completes its first treehouse and ropes course in Fairmount Park. The first Washington DC/Baltimore Annual Dinner is held and raises more than $7,000 in scholarships. The 13th Annual Benefit Dinner and Auction in Portland, ME raises more than $135,000.

1998

Two new courses are run: a 23-day pulling boat/schooner course in Penobscot Bay and a 50-day canoe adventure down the Mistassini, a remote river in Quebec, Canada. The STEP program in Yulee, FL receives "deemed status," an honor that few of the 600 programs in the state of Florida have ever achieved. The school kicks off the $5.5 million 35th Anniversary Campaign to raise money for capital improvements and an increased endowment for staff training and student scholarships.

1999

Wheeler Bay Marina in St. George, ME is purchased to provide a stable mainland base for the Sea Program.

2000

Joan Welsh resigns as president and Jan Hayes, Director of Human Resources, is appointed acting president. Charles "Pen" Williamson, Jr., Vice President of Development, retires after 33 years with the school.

2001

Wheeler Bay opens as the base for the Sea Program. Galen Todd, a 1968 alumnus, is appointed as the fourth president of the School.

2002

Florida "Outreach" is expanded into Charleston, SC with a FINS program. OBP (Outward Bound Professional) builds strength by focusing on repeat, in-depth client relationships incorporating multi-year expeditions for organizational change. Josh L. Miner, who introduced Outward Bound to the United States, dies in January.

2003

The value of Compass programs was recognized when the City of Philadelphia contracted with HIOBS to play a role in the city's efforts to improve their schools. The Board of Trustees voted not to merge HIOBS' Wilderness Programs with Outward Bound West and Voyageur Outward Bound School. The Manatee, FL base is closed.

2004

A summer high school semester course called “Ocean Bound,” a collaboration between HIOBS and the Ocean Classroom Foundation, runs. 20 students board the schooner Westward in Boston and sail for St John, Newfoundland. Then they sea kayak in Newfoundland’s Bay of Exploits for three weeks.  

2005

At the Annual General Meeting and in the face of declining enrollments, HIOBS trustees elect to centralize operations under the national organization, Outward Bound USA.

2006

HIOBS trustees close the administrative offices in Maine and move to affiliate with OBUSA and several other schools. Marketing, development and program operations for the schools are centralized and administered nationally from offices located in Golden, Colorado. The Sea Program ceased operations on Hurricane Island, running courses from Wheeler Bay and maintaining a base site on Burnt Island. In September, the first Adventure Educators’ semester program to run jointly with Unity College begins.

2007

Following a competition for a new vessel for Outward Bound Sea Programs, Roger Martin’s design, the sharpie-schooner design “Hurricane Island 30”, is unanimously chosen from many proposals. The fiberglass design is rigged with two identical spritsails set on unstayed carbon fiber masts. The first two boats of the new design, Redwing and Brimstone, are launched in September from Wheeler Bay.

2008

After many years, the sailing course for deaf students is resurrected in August at Wheeler Bay with The Learning Center. The first courses specifically designed for veterans and returning service members of Operation Iraqi Freedom and Operation Enduring Freedom run in Maine. Intercept, 28-day programs for struggling teens, return to Hurricane Island Outward Bound School.

2009

On April 17, the first Odyssey expedition in over a decade departed Key Largo, FL, bound for Wheeler Bay, Maine. This 50-day staff expedition included 24 experienced instructors on two of the fleet’s new HI-30s, Ritt and Rip Hudner.

2010

The Florida Sea Program and Minnesota Winter Program collaborate on a spring semester, “Dogs to Dolphins,” in which students ski and dogsled for 30 days in the Boundary Waters of northern Minnesota, and then sail in the Florida Keys for 30 days, circumnavigating the southern Florida peninsula.

2011

After six years of shared administration, Hurricane Island Outward Bound and the other U.S. Schools formally affiliated under the national umbrella organization, Outward Bound USA, move to disaffiliate and establish independent Boards of trustees. Eric Denny is appointed Executive Director of Hurricane Island Outward Bound School.

2012

On January 1, the School is re-incorporated as an independent, Maine-based School and officially reclaims the name Hurricane Island Outward Bound School. Twenty-two hundred students are served in open enrollment, group education and Outward Bound professional programs. The year ends with a fiscal surplus for the first time in a decade, made possible by the new and renewed generosity of the School’s many donors.

2013

The Florida Sea Program re-establishes itself in the Lower Keys, with a base on Doctor’s Arm, Big Pine Key, after several years operating out of the FINS base on Key Largo. Long time supporter and trustee Kaki Smith receives the Oar Award in recognition of her outstanding support for HIOBS.

2014

HIOBS celebrates its 50-year anniversary with a benefit dinner, the “Bash,” at Point Lookout on Friday, August 22 and a reunion weekend on Hurricane Island from Saturday, August 23 to Sunday, August 24!
